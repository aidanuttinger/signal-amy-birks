01 · Core Repositioning

From "Change Management" to "Integration Risk Intelligence"

You currently occupy the "change management and culture" category — a space crowded with commoditized practitioners and large consulting firms. The repositioning moves you into a category you can own: the upstream advisor who protects the financial thesis of the deal.

✗ Current Positioning
  • Change management consultant
  • "People and culture" workstream
  • Runs parallel to the integration plan
  • Cost center framing — tolerated by CFOs
  • Competes with Prosci, internal HR, large firms
  • Buyers: HR Directors, Project Managers
✓ New Positioning
  • Integration Risk Intelligence advisor
  • Protects the deal thesis and synergy case
  • Works upstream — before the playbook, not beside it
  • ROI framing — valued by CFOs and deal directors
  • Unclaimed white space — no direct category competitors
  • Buyers: Corporate Dev Directors, CHROs, PE Operating Partners
02 · The USP

The Core Statement That Changes Every Conversation

Primary USP Statement
"I protect the synergy assumptions that justified your deal — by addressing the hidden variable that determines whether your integration plan actually performs as designed."
The Core Thesis (in her words)
"The performance dip that organizations expect during integration isn't inevitable. It's a symptom of unaddressed fear distorting the plan. Address the fear — and you get rocket fuel instead of drag."

The key reframe: Fear is not a soft concept — it's the neurological mechanism that determines decision quality, communication effectiveness, and talent retention during an integration. It has a measurable cost. You are the only person in the market who addresses it at the strategic level rather than the HR level.

03 · Category Design

Owning "Integration Risk Intelligence"

Category design is the highest-leverage positioning move available to an independent advisor. You don't compete in a category — you define one. Here's what that looks like in practice.

🏷️
Name It Consistently
Use "Integration Risk Intelligence" on every surface — website, LinkedIn, proposals, conversations. Repetition creates the category. You are the one who names the thing no one else was naming.
📐
Own the Framework
Integration Intelligence (the methodology) is the proprietary system that delivers Integration Risk Intelligence (the category). The framework is the proof that the category is real and structured.
📊
Speak in Deal Language
Every conversation should anchor to deal value: synergy capture, talent retention, integration timeline. Not "culture" or "engagement." Those are outputs — you are protecting inputs to financial performance.
🎯
Choose the Right Buyers
Corporate Development Directors, PE Operating Partners, CHROs at acquiring orgs. Not HR generalists or project managers. These buyers have budget tied to deal outcomes and understand the risk you are mitigating.

07 · Key Talking Points

Language That Lands With Deal-Makers

These are the phrases that open doors with CFOs and Corporate Development Directors. Use them verbatim until they feel natural.

→
"I protect the deal thesis."
Not "I help with culture." Not "I manage change." This is the opening line that changes the conversation from cost center to risk mitigation.
→
"The performance dip everyone expects isn't inevitable — it's a symptom."
This immediately differentiates from every other practitioner who accepts the dip as given. It's a provocative claim that demands explanation — and you have the explanation.
→
"Fear is running through every line item in your integration plan — whether you're accounting for it or not."
This reframes fear from a soft concept to a structural element that's already in the plan. The question isn't whether to address it — it's whether to address it intentionally.
→
"I work upstream of change management — I'm addressing the conditions that determine whether your integration plan executes as designed."
Positions her as above and before, not beside or below. Creates a new category rather than competing in an existing one.
→
"You paid $X for synergies. Here's what's quietly destroying them."
This is the opening for the diagnostic conversation. Anchors to the deal price, creates urgency, and positions the assessment as insurance, not overhead.
